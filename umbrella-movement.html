The Umbrella Revolution:
Hong Kong's 2014

Pro-Democracy Movement

The Umbrella Revolution, also known as the Umbrella Movement, was a 79-day pro-democracy occupation in Hong Kong demanding genuine universal suffrage.

Protesters shield themselves with umbrellas during a tear gas incident, September 2014.

Background & Causes

Residents were promised eventual universal suffrage under Hong Kong's Basic Law, but leaders continued to be chosen by a small, pro-Beijing committee.

On 31 August 2014, the NPCSC issued the "8.31 decision," requiring candidates to be vetted by a committee, which many viewed as "fake" democracy.

Name & Symbolism

The term emerged from protesters using umbrellas to block police pepper spray and tear gas.

Yellow ribbons and banners reading "I want real universal suffrage" became central visual symbols across Admiralty, Mong Kok, and Causeway Bay.

Key Timeline

22–26 Sept 2014

Students launch class boycotts and enter Civic Square, prompting arrests and concern over police tactics.

28 Sept 2014

Police fire dozens of tear gas canisters; tens of thousands occupy major roads in response.

Oct–Nov 2014

Camps become semi-permanent "cities" with study areas and democracy walls.

11–15 Dec 2014

Authorities clear the final camps, officially ending the 79-day occupation.

Eyewitness Stories

Outcomes & Legacy

The immediate goal of open nominations was not achieved, but the movement fostered a new generation of activists.

The grievances from 2014 laid the groundwork for the 2019 protests, leading to the subsequent National Security Law.
